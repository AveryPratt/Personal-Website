Rocket Calculator

rocketcalculator.azurewebsites.net

Rocket Calculator is a tool I developed that is used to optimize the design of and explore the capabilities of rockets. Ok, so we can all agree rockets are cool, but why would somebody want to go out of their way to design one? Well, that is the exact premise of an incredibly popular simulator game, Kerbal Space Program (KSP). KSP, as well as other similar games such as Orbiter, allow players to to design and test rockets. Once launched, the vehicle's thrust and trajectory adhere to strict Newtonian and Keplerian laws of motion, meaning you can use the same physics equations that real space agencies use to plan their missions.

That's where Rocket Calculator comes in. Many of these simulators, including Kerbal Space Program, provide the player with inadequate information about their rocket design. Unless they have flown a lot of rockets before, they have no way of knowing if their engines are powerful enough to safely land on the moon, or if they have enough fuel to return. Rocket Calculator provides the information necessary to ensure a safe journey, and can also be used to optimize the design to fit a specific mission profile. It can also, of course, be used to calculate the characteristics of real rockets. How large of a payload could SpaceX's Falcon 9 bring to Mars? Which of Jupiter's moons would the Apollo moon lander be able to touch down on? Rocket Calculator can figure it out for you.

Source Code is available on Github.

KSP Library (Framework)

https://github.com/AveryPratt/KSP_Library

I developed KSP Library, a class library, alongside Rocket Calculator to provide myself and others an accessible framework for creating space- or astronomy-related applications in .net. KSP Library contains classes that can be used to easily perform complex calculations to solve problems related to rocketry and orbital physics. Another useful function KSP Library serves is to provide useful information about each planet, moon, or star, or other celestial body in our solar system. This information can be used in many different ways, such as calculating a planet's local gravity, or comparing the mass ratio between a planet and it's moon. Best of all, KSP Library even allows for a user to model their own solar system should they choose to create one.
